**PERSONALITIES**

**Incident**

It was the third week of a module-long group project for our IT Security Project course. Our lecturer had assigned us into groups of five, mixing students from different backgrounds and study tracks. What began as a promising collaboration gradually revealed itself to be one of the most personality-charged experiences I have had in an academic setting.

Our group comprised five very different individuals. There was Kenneth, a fast-talking, ideas-first kind of person who would walk into every meeting with five new suggestions before anyone had even greeted each other properly. He was enthusiastic, charming, and almost exhaustingly energetic. Then there was Seline, quiet and methodical, she rarely spoke in group discussions but would send detailed, colour-coded notes after every session. Opposite her in temperament was Onchwari, who was opinionated, direct, and quick to challenge any idea that he considered half-baked. The fourth member, Mutheu, was our peacemaker, she had an extraordinary ability to sense tension in the room and steer conversations back to calm. And then there was me, somewhere in the middle, trying to understand why our group felt so difficult to navigate despite everyone being genuinely capable.

The breaking point came during our third meeting. Kenneth had proposed a bold, creative angle for our research presentation, using a fictional case study to illustrate real IT Security Project scenario. He was animated, gesturing, selling the idea with infectious energy. Seline said nothing, but her furrowed brow signalled deep discomfort. Onchwari was more direct: "That is not how academic research works. We need evidence, not storytelling." The room tensed immediately. Kenneth fell uncharacteristically quiet. Mutheu attempted a gentle redirect, but the damage was done, the next forty minutes became a stilted exchange where nobody fully committed to any direction.

After the meeting, I sat alone and replayed the scene in my mind. It struck me that nobody in that room had behaved badly. Kenneth was not being reckless; he was being creative. Onchwari was not being unkind; he was being rigorous. Seline was not being dismissive; she was being careful. Mutheu was not being weak; she was being emotionally intelligent. And yet, the collision of these personalities had created genuine dysfunction. The problem was not character; it was personality difference, and none of us had the tools to understand or bridge it.

That meeting became the incident that pushed me to explore the psychology of personality; how individuals differ in their orientations toward the world, and how those differences, when unrecognised, can fracture even the most talented of teams.

**Relation**

The dynamics I observed in that group meeting connect directly to the academic study of personality; the consistent, enduring patterns of thought, emotion, and behaviour that distinguish individuals from one another (Pervin & John, 1999). Personality psychology seeks to understand why people respond to the same situation in markedly different ways, and how these differences can be understood, predicted, and accommodated.

In the context of our group, the friction was not random, it was patterned. Kenneth's enthusiasm and creative impulsiveness mapped closely to high openness and extraversion. Onchwari's directness and focus on evidence reflected high conscientiousness and a preference for structure. Seline's quiet, detail-oriented approach suggested introversion and analytical processing. Mutheu’s role as mediator exemplified high agreeableness. These patterns align almost perfectly with the Big Five personality framework, one of the most widely researched and empirically validated models of personality in psychology.

The incident also connects to the concept of personality-based conflict in groups, a recognised phenomenon in organisational psychology and communication studies. When group members with different personality profiles lack awareness of those differences, misattribution occurs: one person's thoroughness is read as obstruction, another's creativity is dismissed as impracticality. Understanding personality types does not eliminate conflict, but it provides a shared language for navigating it constructively.

Additionally, the experience relates to the concept of psychological safety (Edmondson, 1999), the shared belief within a team that members can take interpersonal risks without fear of judgement. Onchwari's blunt critique, however, analytically sound, disrupted our group's psychological safety. Kenneth's withdrawal following the critique illustrated how personality-driven communication can either build or erode that safety within seconds.

**Research**

The scientific study of personality has a long and rich history. Early theorists such as Hippocrates proposed four temperaments, sanguine, choleric, melancholic, and phlegmatic, as explanations for behavioural differences among people. While this ancient model lacked empirical rigour, it reflected an enduring human intuition: that individuals differ in stable, patterned ways that shape how they engage with the world (Stelmack & Stalikas, 1991).

The most empirically robust contemporary framework is the Big Five personality model, also known as the Five-Factor Model (FFM), developed through decades of factor analytic research by scholars including Tupes and Christal (1961), and later refined by Costa and McCrae (1992). The model identifies five broad personality dimensions: Openness to Experience (intellectual curiosity, creativity, preference for novelty), Conscientiousness (organisation, dependability, goal-directedness), Extraversion (sociability, assertiveness, positive affect), Agreeableness (cooperativeness, empathy, trust), and Neuroticism (emotional instability, anxiety, moodiness). Costa and McCrae's (1992) NEO Personality Inventory remains one of the most widely used and validated tools for measuring these dimensions across cultures and contexts.

Research consistently demonstrates that the Big Five traits are relatively stable across adulthood and have meaningful predictive power for life outcomes. Roberts, Kuncel, Shiner, Caspi, and Goldberg (2007) conducted a meta-analysis showing that personality traits predict important life outcomes including occupational performance, relationship quality, health behaviours, and even longevity, often with effect sizes comparable to or exceeding those of cognitive ability. This finding underscores the practical importance of personality awareness, not merely as an academic exercise, but as a tool for real-world functioning.

Beyond the Big Five, Carl Jung's theory of psychological types, later operationalised in the Myers-Briggs Type Indicator (MBTI) by Briggs and Myers, has had significant influence in applied settings. Jung (1921/1971) proposed that individuals differ along dimensions of introversion-extraversion, sensing-intuition, thinking-feeling, and judging-perceiving. While the MBTI has faced criticism for limited test-retest reliability (Boyle, 1995), its enduring popularity in organisational, educational, and coaching contexts speaks to the human appetite for frameworks that make personality differences legible and discussable.

In group dynamics specifically, personality composition matters significantly. Barrick, Stewart, Neubert, and Mount (1998) studied 51 work teams and found that group-level extraversion and agreeableness positively predicted team performance and viability. Critically, they also found that a single low-agreeableness member could undermine an otherwise high-functioning team, a finding that resonates with the Onchwari dynamic in our group. The research suggests that personality heterogeneity in teams can be a source of creative strength, but only when managed with awareness and intentional communication norms.

Personality also intersects meaningfully with communication style. According to Reeves and Oh (2007), introverted individuals tend to prefer written communication, careful deliberation, and structured settings, a pattern clearly reflected in Seline's behaviour. Extraverts, by contrast, think aloud, prefer verbal exchange, and are energised by group interaction, as exemplified by Kenneth. When teams do not acknowledge these differences, introverts are frequently perceived as disengaged and extraverts as domineering, leading to miscommunication and interpersonal friction that is personality-driven rather than intention-driven.

Psychologist Walter Mischel's (1968) classic challenge to trait theory, known as the person-situation debate, argued that situational factors could override personality traits in shaping behaviour. While Mischel's critique sparked decades of productive debate, contemporary personality psychology has arrived at an interactionist position: personality and situation interact. A highly conscientious person placed in a chaotic group environment will behave differently than in a structured one. This interactionist view is particularly relevant for understanding group behaviour, where situational norms, leadership, and structure either amplify or moderate personality-driven tendencies (Fleeson, 2004).

Finally, research on personality and emotional intelligence (EI) adds an important layer. Petrides and Furnham (2001) found that trait EI, the disposition to perceive, manage, and use emotions effectively, acts as a buffer in personality-diverse groups. Individuals high in trait EI, like Mutheu in our group, are better equipped to recognise the emotional undercurrents of personality differences and respond adaptively. This finding suggests that developing emotional intelligence is one of the most practical responses to navigating personality diversity in professional and academic settings.

**Reflection**

Sitting with this incident and working through the research behind it has shifted something fundamental in how I understand myself and the people around me. I came into that group project thinking that team difficulties were a matter of effort or attitude, that if people simply tried harder to cooperate, collaboration would follow. What I understand now is that personality differences create very real, very structural challenges that effort alone cannot resolve.

The most significant new insight I have gained is the concept of personality-driven miscommunication. Before engaging with this research, I would have described Onchwari as blunt, Kenneth as scattered, and Seline as distant. These were moral-adjacent labels, they carried implicit judgements. Now I understand that these three individuals were expressing their personalities: Onchwari's conscientiousness made him quick to scrutinise; Kenneth's openness made him quick to imagine; Seline's introversion made her cautious about voicing incomplete thoughts. None of these are character flaws. They are personality orientations. and recognising that difference is profound.

I also gained an entirely new appreciation for agreeableness as a dimension of group intelligence, not mere niceness. Barrick et al.'s (1998) finding that one low-agreeableness member can compromise an entire team, reframed how I think about group composition. And Mutheu’s role in our group, which I had previously taken for granted as simply "being nice," I now recognise as the exercise of high trait emotional intelligence, a genuinely rare and valuable professional capacity (Petrides & Furnham, 2001).

The interactionist perspective from Fleeson (2004) also challenged a prior assumption: that personality is destiny. Understanding that personality and situation interact means that how a group structures its meetings, establishes its norms, and communicates its expectations can either moderate or amplify personality-driven conflict. This gives me genuine agency. I cannot change my teammates' personalities, but I can advocate for structures, ground rules, rotating facilitation, written pre-meeting proposals, that create space for different personality types to contribute meaningfully.

What is entirely new for me is thinking about personality not just as something I have, but as something I bring into every room, and that others do too. Every interaction is a personality encounter. Every group is a personality ecosystem. Going forward, I intend to use my knowledge of the Big Five not to typecast people, but to widen my interpretive lens: to ask, before judging, whether what I am reading as resistance might be conscientiousness, whether what I am reading as aloofness might be introversion, whether what seems like recklessness might be creative openness. That shift in perception, from judgement to curiosity, is perhaps the most practically transformative thing this incident has taught me.
